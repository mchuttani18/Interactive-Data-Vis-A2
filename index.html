Subtheme: Making a Case for Upzoning

Overall Analysis Questions

As a Massachusetts native growing up in suburb with significant exclusionary zoning, I regularly heard neighbors advocate against development to maintain the “character” of our town - phrases that, as Boston Indicators writes, often masks more insidious policies of “class and race discrimination” (Exclusionary by Design Report, 2023, p. 9). Zoning is often used as a tool of oppression, exclusion and segregation in Massachusetts. Last week, Dedham, despite allowing multi-family zoning, denied a zoning variance that would have allowed for an abandoned restaurant to be converted into a food distribution center and housing supports for migrants seeking asylum (Boston Globe, 2024). An anti-immigrant resident coalition fought the variance highlighted by raising “traffic”, “loitering” and “safety” as concerns - racialized, xenophobic and bigoted sentiments masked under a facade of economic well being.

Exclusionary zoning not only limits the life chances of low-income, immigrant, BIPOC, and other people who face structural racism, classism, and xenophobia - it also strips away at the empathy, humanity, and wellbeing of wealthier residents who live in these towns. Exclusionary zoning policies are not only inhumane, but also economic drains that waste time and resources in places like my hometown that face multi-year zoning litigations and appeals when residents fight against free meal sites, healthcare centers, and dignified shelter. In public schools, exclusionary zoning limits the possibility for children of all income levels to meet, exchange ideas, customs, and perspectives, and develop a shared sense of identity with other like-minded classmates that extends beyond class lines.

In data visualizations, it’s common that wealthy suburbs are lauded for their high household income or elevated SAT scores, which then in turn encourages exclusionary zoning which can boost these metrics. In this assignment, I’d like to begin highlighting the harms of exclusionary zoning instead by exploring metrics such as zoning’s impacts on racial diversity (Question 2), economic diversity (Question 3), and geography (Question 1).

  1. Which towns and cities in Greater Boston have the highest rates of exclusionary zoning?
  2. How does exclusionary zoning impact the racial diversity of municipalities?
  3. How does exclusionary zoning impact the distribution of low, middle, and high income households?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

This exploratory analysis showed stark differences between tracts with inclusive and exclusive zoning practices across most towns in the Greater Boston area. Black, Asian and Latinx residents were far more likely to live in multi-family zones than their White counterparts. While high-income residents were split between living in single and multi-family zones, only 15% of residents with very low incomes (<$10,000 per household) lived in single-family zones.

Despite these trends, significant outliers exist - towns like Wayland that have few low-income families despite its inclusionary zoning policies, or Randolph which is welcoming to many low-income families despite its city-wide single-family zoning. These outliers warrant further investigation as to the social, political, cultural, and economic forces that drive continued separation by income.
